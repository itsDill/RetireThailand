Banking in Thailand

Bank accounts, money transfers, and managing finances as an expat

üè¶ Banking Overview for Expats

Thailand has a modern banking system, but opening an account as a foreigner requires some preparation. Here's what you need to know.

üèß

ATM Access

ATMs everywhere accepting international cards. 220 THB fee per foreign withdrawal. Daily limits 20,000-30,000 THB per transaction.

üí≥

Card Payments

Credit/debit cards widely accepted in cities. Cash still king in markets, small shops, and rural areas. QR payments popular.

üì±

Mobile Banking

Thai banks have excellent apps with English. Transfer money, pay bills, top up phones instantly. Very convenient once set up.

üí±

Currency

Thai Baht (THB). ~35 THB = $1 USD. Exchange rates better in Thailand than abroad. Super Rich exchanges offer best rates.

üìã Opening a Thai Bank Account

Having a Thai bank account makes life much easier‚Äîpay rent, utilities, and receive money without fees.

What You'll Need

‚úÖ Essential Documents

  • Passport (original + copies)
  • Valid visa (Non-O, O-A, O-X, etc.)
  • Proof of address (rental contract or utility bill)
  • Work permit OR Retirement letter from embassy OR Bank reference letter

üí° Helpful to Have

  • Thai phone number (required for SMS/app)
  • Reference letter from home bank
  • Minimum deposit (500-2,000 THB)
  • Letter from condo/landlord
1

Choose Your Bank

Bangkok Bank most expat-friendly. Kasikorn (K-Bank) good app. SCB has English support. Visit main branches, not mall kiosks.

2

Visit in Person

Go to a main branch (not sub-branch) with all documents. Morning visits best. Bring Thai speaker if possible.

3

Complete Application

Fill out forms, provide documents, get photographed. Process takes 30-60 minutes if approved.

4

Receive Bankbook & Card

Get passbook immediately. ATM/debit card issued on spot or mailed. Request internet/mobile banking setup.

‚ö†Ô∏è Account Opening Tips

  • Tourist visas: Most banks won't open accounts on tourist visas. Some exceptions exist but rare.
  • Branch varies: Policies vary by branch and staff. If refused, try another branch or bank.
  • Embassy letter: Get a "Certificate of Residence" from your embassy‚Äîvery helpful for banking.
  • Be patient: Process can be frustrating. Stay calm, be polite, and bring all documents.

üåü Why Wise is Recommended

Most expats in Thailand use Wise (formerly TransferWise) for regular transfers:

  • Mid-market exchange rate (no hidden markup)
  • Low transparent fees (~0.5-1%)
  • Fast delivery (usually 1-2 business days)
  • Easy app, track transfers in real-time
  • Can send directly to Thai bank account
  • Wise debit card option for multi-currency

üí° Pro Tip: FETF for Property Purchase

If buying a condo, you MUST transfer funds from abroad in foreign currency and get a Foreign Exchange Transaction Form (FETF) from your Thai bank. This proves funds came from overseas‚Äîrequired for foreign ownership.

üìä Tax Considerations

Understanding your tax obligations in Thailand and your home country.

Thai Tax Basics

  • Tax residency: 180+ days in Thailand = Thai tax resident
  • Income tax: Progressive 0-35% on Thai-sourced income
  • Pension income: Generally not taxed if earned abroad (check treaties)
  • Remitted income: New 2024 rules may tax foreign income brought into Thailand
  • Tax year: Calendar year (Jan-Dec)

2024 Tax Changes

  • Thailand now taxes foreign income remitted in the same year earned
  • Previously, only income remitted in the year earned was taxed
  • Impact on retirees varies by country and income type
  • Double Tax Agreements (DTAs) may provide relief
  • Consult a Thai tax advisor for your situation

Home Country Obligations

  • US citizens: Must file US taxes worldwide, FBAR if $10K+ in foreign accounts
  • UK citizens: May remain UK tax resident, check rules
  • Australians: Check residency status, may have obligations
  • Most countries: Losing tax residency reduces obligations
  • Always: Consult tax professional in BOTH countries

‚ö†Ô∏è Important Tax Notes

  • Tax laws are complex and change frequently. This is general guidance only.
  • Your situation depends on: citizenship, income sources, residency status, tax treaties.
  • Hire a qualified tax advisor who understands both Thai and your home country tax law.
  • Keep good records of all income, transfers, and time spent in each country.

üí° Practical Money Tips

üí≥ Get a No-Fee Card

Before coming, get a card with no foreign transaction fees (Charles Schwab, Wise, Revolut). Saves 2-3% on every purchase.

üèß ATM Strategy

Withdraw max amount (30,000 THB) to minimize 220 THB fees. Decline "conversion" offers‚Äîalways choose THB. Use Aeon ATMs (no Thai fee).

üíµ Keep USD/EUR Cash

Keep some home currency cash for emergencies. Exchange at Super Rich or similar (not airports/hotels). Better rates than ATM for large amounts.

üì± Thai QR Payments

Once you have a Thai bank account, use PromptPay QR for payments. Instant, free, accepted everywhere including markets.

üí∞ Monthly Budget Transfer

Set up regular Wise transfers for monthly expenses. Keeps finances organized and takes advantage of good rates automatically.

üîê Keep Home Account

Maintain bank account in home country for pension deposits, investments, and backup. Don't close everything before moving.
